    In this article, Ludovico and Cramer discuss the state of print in the digital age. I found their advocacy for a hybrid print style -- in which both analog and digital print styles are preserved -- particularly interesting. For example, having a QR code on a print magazine that updates periodically: users can return to the same print magazine from time to time to get updates. I also think they bring up an important point about free print: there should be standards around digital print that protect artists from the platforms they produce their work on. There is also the interesting (but in my opinion, somewhat conservative) argument that digital print should not simply digitize analog print, but do something innovative (but personally, I think that's fine). Finally, I think the authors bring up the important point that there are just some things digital prints do better than analog prints (e.g. network effects) but there are some things that analog print has that digital print cannot replicate (the tactile feel of flipping through pages).

    1. What is the value of analog print in the digital age? Is it now the domain of enthusiasts?

  • Week 10 Reading

    Week 10's selection concerns a language that every human on Earth "speaks", the visual language. Unlike traditional languages, which are literal and sequential, the visual language is perceived and simultaneous. And there exists a framework for understanding this language: Gestalt psychology. This psychology posits that human beings view the world in wholes and not constituent parts, discovering that we can be "tricked" into believing certain things, or having certain experiences, through clever organization of visual forms. If the visual language is a language, we can break it down into words, syntax, and grammar. Words: shapes, like squares, circles, and triangles. Syntax: how we organize these shapes into visual forms. And grammar: how we ensure that this organization is (by some defined rules) correct. I had to drive during my recent spring break trip to Mexico, and, despite not speaking an ounce of Spanish, knew that a red octagon with "ALTO" in capital letters, placed on the right side of the street, (probably!) meant stop. And herein exists a language, a language that I understood without needing to know Spanish, composed of a red octagon, placed on the right side of my field of vision, that I understood because it was (grammatically) correct. The article ends with the statement: "if only we could see a photograph of the whole world". The problem is, we perceive the 3D world in 2D. We would have to transcend to some higher dimension to see our 3D world. And I think that's fascinating, despite being physically impossible.

    1. If we can think of the visual language as a language, what do new words look like? How are advancements in technology adding new words to our visual language?
    2. How can we encourage accessibility in our websites by employing visual language? Is it possible to design a website anyone can perceptually understand?
  • Week 12 Reading

    Dot Tuer's exploration in "Beyond the New Media Frame: The Poetics of Absence in Vera Frenkel's String Games" reveals an intricate intersection of technology, performance art, and memory. Frenkel's pioneering teleconferencing piece, String Games (1974), utilized emerging video technology to explore connections between participants in separate cities, metaphorically bridging physical distance with conceptual intimacy. Tuer illustrates how Frenkel's innovative approach, characterized by "multiple narratives, multiple exchanges, and open-ended outcomes," challenges traditional notions of presence, highlighting instead a poignant poetics of absence. Central to this poetics is Frenkel's symbolic manipulation of absence, where the virtual string game Cat's Cradle metaphorically represents human connections and communication breakdowns simultaneously enabled and limited by technological mediation. This motif becomes a recurring thematic anchor in Frenkel's oeuvre, manifesting deeply in later works such as the Body Missing website (1995). Here, the digital platform itself becomes a means to probe historical traumas, memory, and loss. Tuer emphasizes how Frenkel consistently questions the role of technology, not merely as a facilitator of interactivity but as a critical lens revealing the ephemeral and often elusive nature of human presence and historical remembrance.

    1. How does Vera Frenkel's approach to interactivity in "String Games" redefine our understanding of digital communication and presence?
    2. In what ways can contemporary artists continue to explore the "poetics of absence" within today's rapidly evolving digital and media landscapes?
